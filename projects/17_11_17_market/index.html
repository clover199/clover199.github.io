This is a introduction of market theories based on the course I took this semester and mixed with my own understandings. There are three parts in this post: linear markets, solving linear markets with Lemke algorithm, and welfare theorems.

Market

We work to earn money; and we use money to buy what we need. There are a lot of products in the market. How does the market set prices for each product? A quick thinker may say, products with higher demands will become more expensive. Another may say, rare items like diamonds always have high prices.

Proposed by Irving Fisher in 1891 in his Ph.D. thesis, a Fisher market has \(m\) agents and \(n\) of divisible goods. As shown in the above cartoon, each agent comes with a budget of \(M_i\) dollars and wants to buy some goods (preferences over different goods are indicated by the width of the line). Of course, no agent will be satisfied if we give them goods randomly. Suppose agent \(i\) is given a bundle of goods \(x_i=\{x_{i1},\dots,x_{in}\}\). He uses a number \(U_i(x_i)\) to indicate his satisfactory over this bundle. This number is called the utility of this agent. We assume we know the utility of all agents.

Appendix

A:

Suppose \(P\) is a functional of \(\{x_i\}\). We want to minimize $$G = \sum_{\{x_i\}} P E + T \sum_{\{x_i\}} P \ln P$$ under constraint \(h = \sum_{\{x_i\}} P -1\). Using Lagrange multiplier, we transform the problem to be minimizing \(\tilde{G}=G+\lambda h\) without constraints. At minimum, the variant of \(G\) under variant of \(P\) should be zero $$\frac{\delta\tilde{G}}{\delta P} = E + T (\ln P +1) + \lambda = 0 $$ The derivative with respect to \(\lambda\) gives back us the original constraint $$ \frac{\partial\tilde{G}}{\partial\lambda} = \sum_{\{x_i\}} P - 1 = 0$$ Solve the two equations above, we get $$ P = \frac{1}{Z} e^{-E/T} $$ which is Boltzmann distribution. \(\lambda\) is chosen to satisfy the normalization condition and is absorbed into \(Z\).
